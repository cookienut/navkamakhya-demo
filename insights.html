Industry Insights

The state of the global seafarer workforce

Crewing decisions are easier with real numbers behind them. Below is a short, sourced summary of where the global and Indian seafarer workforce stand today — no projections presented as fact, every figure attributed.

Global Supply & Demand

A widening officer shortage

The Seafarer Workforce Report 2026, published jointly by BIMCO and the International Chamber of Shipping (ICS) and released around the Day of the Seafarer in June 2026, is the industry's benchmark study on seafarer supply and demand. Its headline findings:

2.57M

seafarers are currently estimated to be serving the global fleet, operating 85,148 merchant ships worldwide.

39,100

is the estimated 2026 shortfall of STCW-certified officers, alongside a projected surplus of 56,890 ratings.

113,735

more officers will be needed by 2030 to crew the world merchant fleet as demand keeps outpacing supply.

+35%

is the rise in demand for STCW-certified seafarers since the previous report in 2021.

Source: BIMCO & International Chamber of Shipping, Seafarer Workforce Report 2026 (published June 2026).

What This Means For Crewing

Why the shortage matters to shipowners

An officer shortfall of this size means competition for qualified seafarers is only going to intensify. BIMCO's own commentary on the report frames retention and pipeline-building as urgent, not optional, tasks for the industry — a candidate bench that was "good enough" two years ago may already be too thin for 2026 crewing volumes. For shipowners, that argues for crewing partners who are actively sourcing and pre-verifying candidates rather than reacting to vacancies as they appear.

India's Role

India as a seafarer-supplying nation

Among the top 3 globally

India ranks among the top three countries in the world by number of seafarers supplied to the global merchant fleet, per statements from the Ministry of Ports, Shipping & Waterways.

Workforce more than doubled

India's seafarer workforce has grown from roughly 1.25 lakh (125,000) a decade ago to over 3 lakh (300,000) seafarers today, according to the Ministry.

On the IMO White List

India's presence on the IMO White List confirms its training and certification framework is recognised as fully compliant with the STCW Convention and Code — a factor that supports Indian seafarers' international employability.

Sources: Ministry of Ports, Shipping & Waterways, Government of India (statements by Union Minister Sarbananda Sonowal, 2025–26); Marine Insight, Top 10 Seafarer-Supplying Nations.
